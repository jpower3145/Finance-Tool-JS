Help & Advice

Cash

The word cash can be misconstrued as a box under your bed with wads of physical cash. However cash also means anything held with a physical and non-physical bank that isn't invested in anything else. A common example is people put excess cash in savings accounts where they earn interest that is approximately the Bank of England base rate. Cash can also simply be the possibly stagnant amount kept in a main bank account as well.

Stocks and Shares

Stocks

A diverse umbrella term for something that is purchasable (via stock exchanges) to facilitate ownership in a specific or range of companies.

Shares

A metric for how a certain stock is divided. For example if you own 15 shares of Apple you consequently own around 1 billionth of the stock.

Defined Contribution Pensions

Defined Contribution is used for the majority of workplace pensions and involves salary sacrificed or net contributions to fund an investment in a certain product that ultimately aims for long-term growth. Lots of risk-averse investors believe they are market detatched but under the hood of their workplace investment scheme it is heavily determined by the stock market.

Example: If you earn £35,000 and contribute 5% (£1,750), your employer might chip in a mandatory 3% (£1,050). Money put into DC pensions is also NOT taxed on the way in so avoids income tax [and some National Insurance if salary sacrifice is used] which means contributions can often double in effective value compared to hitting your bank account. This is before any market-based growth is considered.

Defined Benefit Pensions

Defined benefit pensions are less common today and can be confusing, but they guarantee a predictable annual income for life after retirement. Typically, these are state- or council-backed and are common across the public sector.

As an example, the Teachers' Pension Scheme uses a 1/57th career-average benefit structure. Members sacrifice between 7.4% and 12% of their pay each year. In return, for every year they work, they "bank" a guaranteed annual retirement income equal to that year's salary divided by 57 (which is then topped up by inflation-linked interest every year until retirement).

Example: If your salary is £35,000 in 2026, you sacrifice 7.4% (£2,590). In return, you guarantee yourself £614 of annual income (£35,000 / 57) for every year you are alive after reaching your State Pension Age, based on that single year of work alone.
